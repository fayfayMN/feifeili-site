🌏 About Me

Teacher. Interpreter. Mail carrier. Builder. The path wasn't linear — that's the point.

I've spent twenty years learning how people communicate under pressure — in Zhejiang classrooms, American hospital & clinic rooms, and Minnesota mail routes at −20°F OR 90°F above. Now I build data and AI tools — like TeamUp and Team Doctor — for the same thing: clarity when it matters.

🧭 The Arc

I was a middle school social science teacher in Zhejiang, China — my first lesson in how structure shapes what people can learn. I became a Mandarin medical interpreter in American clinics and hospitals, showing up for patients in the hardest moments of their lives. I delivered mail through Minnesota winters with a podcast in one ear and the cold in my bones, learning that precision and stamina are the same muscle.

When I moved to the United States, much of my prior academic work didn't transfer — so I started over. I earned a Computer Science associate degree with honors, and I'm now a Data Science student at Metropolitan State University graduating Dec 2026 with a 4.00 local GPA and a 3.91 cumulative. I'm the kind who ships products instead of just talking about them.

Every chapter taught me something different. The throughline: structure helps when words don't.

💡 Why Data Science

Medical interpretation taught me that a single wrong word can change a diagnosis. Data science is the same craft at scale — making sure the signal gets through clean, so the people on the other end can act on it.

I'm drawn to problems where language, healthcare, and human decisions meet data. That's where my background isn't a detour — it's the whole point.

🧠 How I Think

I see organizations as systems. When teams fail, it's almost never because the people are wrong — it's because the structure can't hold what the people bring. My work focuses on the smallest possible system that makes a team functional: clear ownership, written decisions, mutual visibility.

Not enterprise complexity. Just enough structure to let humans work well together.

Frameworks I reach for

  • RACI charts — ambiguity about who decides is the root of most team dysfunction.
  • Decision logs — written decisions compound; unwritten ones evaporate.
  • Operating charters — every team needs a constitution, even a one-paragraph one.
  • Contribution logs — make work visible, especially the quiet work.

🛡️ What I've Learned the Hard Way

The kind that stick.

  • Clear structure prevents conflict better than good intentions do. A simple RACI chart or decision log upfront saves weeks of confusion later.
  • How you deliver work matters as much as the work itself. I've learned to pace delivery to match the team's capacity to absorb.
  • Forward-looking process beats backward-looking blame. The useful question isn't "who was responsible?" — it's "how do we decide differently starting now?"
  • Document for the next person, not for yourself. Good documentation isn't a receipt — it's a gift to whoever picks up the work after you.
  • Adapt your approach to the team you have, not the team you imagined. The best process doesn't matter if it doesn't fit the people in the room.
